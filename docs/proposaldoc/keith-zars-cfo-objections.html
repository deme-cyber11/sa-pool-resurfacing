Hard objections — CFO instinct, need a real answer
Billing trust
How do I know you won't just invoice us for jobs we didn't close?

You're the one who triggers the invoice. We never send a bill until you reply to the lead email with the project address and signed date. There's no audit, no guessing — if you don't report it, you don't get invoiced. The close-report system is honor-based and designed so that paying is always the rational choice: you only pay on jobs where you've already collected money from the homeowner.

Cost comparison
Our Google Ads cost is way less than $500 per lead.

We're $500 per closed job, not per lead. If you pay $50/lead on Google with a 10% close rate, that's $500 per closed job — identical. If your Google close rate is lower than 10%, this channel is actually cheaper. The difference: your Google cost is variable and retroactive. Our rate is fixed and only triggered on value received. At 25% close rate, your cost-per-job is $2,000 through Google vs. $500 here.

Exclusivity
What stops you from selling the same lead to five other pool companies?

The agreement is explicit: once delivered, we don't sell, transfer, or share that lead with any other party. We can't prevent a homeowner from independently calling other contractors, but our distribution is exclusive to Keith Zars. If we were caught sharing leads, the relationship and our reputation both end — we have no incentive to cheat an arrangement that's good for both sides.

→ Agreement Clause 4 (Exclusivity)
Liability exposure
What's our maximum financial exposure here? What if this goes sideways?

Zero retainer, zero setup fee, zero minimum commitment. Your only financial exposure is $500 per job you choose to report as closed — and that's only after you've collected from the homeowner. Our liability to you is capped at dispute credits (Clause 11). Worst case: you receive leads, none close, you owe nothing. Best case: you're paying $500 on jobs that bill $50k–$150k.

Medium objections — process questions, clear answers
Payment terms
Net-15 is too tight for our AP cycle. We need Net-30.

Net-15 is the default — it's negotiable before you sign. Just note your preference in writing when you return the agreement and we'll adjust the clause. The late-interest provision (1.5%/mo after 30 days) would shift accordingly. We've worked Net-30 with larger contractors without issue.

→ Agreement Clause 6 — amend before signing
Volume risk
What if lead volume is low — like 2 leads a month? We can't build a pipeline on that.

The site is live and ranking — lead flow is organic, not guaranteed volume. But there's a built-in protection: if volume drops to zero for 60 consecutive days, you can terminate immediately with no notice period required. And since there's no retainer, a slow month costs you nothing. You're not betting a monthly fee on volume.

→ Handling Doc Section 9 (zero-volume exit)
Exit terms
What if we want to get out? Are we locked into a contract?

Month-to-month. 30 days written notice. No penalties. Email us tomorrow and you're out in 30 days. The only obligations that survive termination are invoices for leads already in your pipeline that close during the notice period — jobs you chose to take and close. No wind-down fees, no early-termination clauses.

→ Agreement Clause 10 (Term & Termination)
Asset ownership
If this works really well, can we eventually buy the site?

The site stays with Iron Tiger Digital — that's the model. You're paying for results, not infrastructure. We handle SEO, hosting, content, and ranking upkeep indefinitely. If you owned the site, you'd be managing all of that. Termination grants no claim to the domain or rankings (Clause 4). The good news: if you exit, you take your homeowner data with you — it's yours within the confidentiality terms.

→ Agreement Clause 4 (last sentence)
Easy objections — soft resistance, flip quickly
Status quo
We already have a full pipeline from Google and referrals. We don't need more leads.

This is purely additive. No retainer means zero fixed cost when leads don't flow. If your sales team is at capacity, you don't have to close every lead — you can be selective. You only pay when you choose to report a close. It's a second channel with no overhead on your end and no obligation to chase every inquiry you receive.

Vendor overhead
We don't want to manage another vendor relationship. We're stretched thin.

The operational overhead is minimal by design. No portal. No app. No account setup. Leads arrive via email. When a job closes, you reply to that email with two sentences. Disputes are a reply to the same thread. Billing disputes go to one email address. The entire relationship runs through your inbox — no new tools required.

Lead validity
What happens if we get a garbage lead — wrong number, out of area?

We validate before delivery — incomplete or out-of-territory submissions don't reach you. If a bad one slips through, reply to the lead email within 14 calendar days with the reason. We review in 2 business days and credit your next invoice if the dispute is valid. Valid grounds: wrong/disconnected number, address outside territory, exact duplicate, obvious spam. Not valid: lead didn't answer, changed their mind, or chose a competitor.

→ Agreement Clause 7 + Handling Doc Section 7
Billing reconciliation
How do we reconcile what we actually owe at the end of the month?

We provide a shared read-only lead log on request — every lead delivered with timestamp, name, project type, and status. Cross-reference it against your CRM's Iron Tiger Digital-tagged deals. Each invoice maps to a specific lead ID. If there's ever a discrepancy, email with the invoice number and we resolve within 10 business days.

Closing the CFO

If they're still hesitant after addressing objections

  • 1 Use the zero-downside frame. "Walk me through the scenario where you come out worse off. You receive leads, some close — you pay $500 per closed job on $50k+ projects. If none close, you owe nothing. What's the risk you're trying to protect against?" Make them articulate it. Usually they can't.
  • 2 Reference the ROI math they already know. "You're already spending on Google. What does your current cost-per-closed-job look like? If it's above $500, this channel is additive and cheaper." CFOs respond to numbers they already own.
  • 3 Offer a pilot framing. "Sign month-to-month, see how the first 30–60 days of leads perform. If it's not working, 30 days notice and you're out clean." Removes the commitment fear without giving up anything.
  • 4 Don't negotiate the $500 rate. The rate is set because a pool job is $50k–$150k. Discounting signals the leads aren't worth the ask. Hold the rate; negotiate terms (Net-30, territory, dispute window) if needed.
  • 5 Get verbal yes, then route to signature. "If the terms work for you, I can have the agreement in your inbox today. DocuSign — takes 2 minutes." Don't end the call on a 'think about it.' Get a clear next step.
Don't over-negotiate against yourself. This CFO's job is to find risk. Your job is to show there isn't any. Every objection above has a clean answer — use it, then be quiet. The silence after a good answer is where the yes lives.
